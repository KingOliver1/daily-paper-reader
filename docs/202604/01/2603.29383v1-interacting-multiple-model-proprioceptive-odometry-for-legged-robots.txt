# Interacting Multiple Model Proprioceptive Odometry for Legged Robots 

Wanlei Li, Zichang Chen, Shilei Li, Xiaogang Xiong, Yunjiang Lou . 

Abstract ‚ÄîState estimation for legged robots remains chal-lenging because legged odometry generally suffers from limited observability and therefore depends critically on measurement constraints to suppress drift. When exteroceptive sensors are unreliable or degraded, such constraints are mainly derived from proprioceptive measurements, particularly leg kinematics and contact information. However, most existing proprioceptive odometry methods rely on an idealized point-contact assumption, which is often violated during real locomotion on complex terrain. Consequently, the effectiveness of proprioceptive constraints may be significantly reduced, resulting in degraded estimation accu-racy. To address these limitations, we propose an interacting mul-tiple model (IMM)-based proprioceptive odometry framework for legged robots. By incorporating multiple contact hypotheses within a unified probabilistic framework, the proposed method enables online mode switching and probabilistic fusion under varying contact conditions. Extensive simulations and real-world experiments demonstrate that the proposed method achieves superior pose estimation accuracy over state-of-the-art methods while maintaining comparable computational efficiency. 

Index Terms ‚ÄîLegged Robots, Proprioceptive Sensors, Inter-acting Multiple Model. 

I. I NTRODUCTION 

# LEGGED robots have been widely deployed in complex and unstructured environments due to their ability to traverse uneven terrain. To achieve reliable locomotion in such environments, accurate state estimation is essential. Conse-quently, a wide range of state estimation algorithms have been developed using different sensing modalities [1]‚Äì[5]. These ap-proaches typically rely on either proprioceptive sensors, such as inertial measurement units (IMU), joint encoders, torque and force sensors, or exteroceptive sensors, including cameras, LiDAR, and global positioning systems (GPS) systems. Although fusing proprioceptive and exteroceptive sensors can significantly improve estimation accuracy, exteroceptive measurements are often degraded or unavailable in real-world scenarios. For example, vision-based sensors are sensitive to reflective or transparent surfaces, lighting variations, motion blur, and environmental occlusions. These limitations motivate the development of state estimation methods that rely solely on proprioceptive sensing, which provides a robust baseline when exteroceptive information is absent or corrupted.

Among proprioceptive sensing modalities, IMUs and joint encoders form the foundation of most proprioceptive odometry (PO) methods for legged robots. Existing PO approaches mainly include optimization-based [1], [2], learning-based [6], [7], and filtering-based frameworks. Among them, filtering-based methods [4], [8] are particularly attractive for real-time legged locomotion because they offer a favorable balance between estimation accuracy and computational efficiency. However, their effectiveness depends strongly on the validity of the assumed foot-ground contact model. Most existing proprioceptive odometry methods rely on a static point-contact assumption. In practice, foot-ground interactions are often non-ideal and time-varying under com-plex terrain conditions. The robot foot may deviate from the idealized point-contact behavior, and slippage may occur under insufficient friction or external disturbances. As a re-sult, the proprioceptive constraints constructed from a single fixed contact model become unreliable, significantly degrading estimation accuracy. These limitations motivate the use of a multiple-hypothesis estimation framework that can infer and fuse different contact conditions in a unified probabilistic manner. To address these challenges, this paper proposes an inter-acting multiple model (IMM)-based proprioceptive odometry framework for legged robots. The proposed method enables online inference and probabilistic fusion of multiple contact hypotheses under varying foot-ground interaction conditions. In addition, a rolling-aware estimation design is introduced by incorporating foot velocity into the estimator state, allowing the estimator to better account for contact-induced motion deviations using only proprioceptive sensing. The main con-tributions of this work are summarized as follows:  

> ‚Ä¢

We formulate proprioceptive odometry for legged robots under dynamic foot‚Äìground interaction as a multiple-contact hypothesis estimation problem, and develop an IMM-based filtering framework for online contact-mode inference and fusion.  

> ‚Ä¢

We propose a rolling-aware state augmentation by incor-porating foot velocity into the estimator state, enabling explicit modeling of contact-induced motion deviations using only proprioceptive measurements.  

> ‚Ä¢

Extensive simulations and real-world experiments demon-strate that the proposed rolling-aware multi-model esti-mator improves accuracy and robustness under slippery and challenging contact conditions, while outperform-ing state-of-the-art proprioceptive methods at comparable computational cost.

The remainder of this paper is organized as follows: Sec. II reviews related work; Sec. III introduces the proposed propri-oceptive odometry; Sec. IV presents experimental validation; and Sec. V summarizes our contributions. II. R ELATED WORK 

For legged robot state estimation, a widely adopted ap-proach is to fuse inertial and kinematic information using low-cost proprioceptive sensors such as IMU and joint encoders. In this context, proprioceptive odometry (PO) has emerged as a dominant paradigm, enabling continuous state estimation using only inertial and kinematic measurements. For example, Bloesch et al. [8] proposed an Extended Kalman filter (EKF)-based state estimator for quadruped robots, which incorporates leg kinematics and contact constraints into the observation model. This framework was later implemented on the MIT Cheetah 3 robot using a linear Kalman filter formulation [9]. Although these approaches demonstrate observability of the body position, velocity, roll, and pitch, their convergence may be slow due to linearization errors and mismatched error distributions. Hartley et al. [4] later reformulated the estimator as an invariant extended Kalman filter (IEKF), deriving the estimation equations directly on the Lie group manifold and significantly improving consistency and convergence proper-ties. More recently, similar estimation principles have been extended to learning-based approaches [6], [7]. However, such methods typically rely on large-scale training datasets and lack guaranteed robustness in previously unseen scenarios. To enhance robustness and long-term accuracy, another line of research introduces exteroceptive sensors, such as cameras and LiDAR, which provide additional environmen-tal constraints. For example, Wisth et al. [1] developed a tightly coupled visual‚Äìinertial‚Äìleg odometry system based on factor graphs, incorporating kinematic velocity measurements. In subsequent work [2], LiDAR sensing was further inte-grated into the framework, enabling reliable state estimation across diverse and challenging terrains. However, as mentioned above, these exteroceptive sensors may be sensitive to environ-mental conditions in degenerate scenarios, potentially leading to decreased accuracy or even complete failure in challenging environments. As a result, purely proprioceptive state estima-tion remains an essential and indispensable solution for legged robots operating in such conditions. However, kinematic‚Äìinertial methods based solely on pro-prioceptive sensing typically assume that the contact location of a stance foot remains static throughout the stance phase. This assumption is often violated in practice due to dynamic foot‚Äìground contact, leading to link deformation and slippage, which are major sources of leg odometry drift [10]. To address errors induced by link deformation, several works explicitly account for kinematic model uncertainties within the estimation framework. For instance, Agrawal et al. [11] incorporated uncertainty in the kinematic chain to reduce estimation errors caused by structural deformation. Similarly, [12] proposed an online link calibration approach to mitigate positional drift arising from time-varying kinematic parameters under dynamic contact conditions. In addition to link deformation, foot‚Äìground slip further violates the static contact assumption and degrades state es-timation accuracy. To mitigate this issue, Bloesch et al. [3] proposed a UKF that fuses IMU and kinematic velocity measurements, where a Mahalanobis-distance threshold on the filter innovation is used to reject unreliable velocity observa-tions. This idea was later extended by Kim et al. [13], who instead used a tunable threshold on the estimated contact foot velocity. Wisth et al. [2] modeled dynamic contact as a bias term in the linear velocity measurement from leg odometry. Yang et al. [14] further investigated rolling contact effects by embedding multiple IMUs along the leg to measure the an-gular velocity induced by foot rolling, although this approach requires additional sensing hardware. To handle systems with switching dynamics, the interacting multiple model (IMM) framework has been widely applied in robotics and other dynamic systems [15], [16]. By maintaining multiple candidate models and recursively updating their posterior probabilities online, IMM enables effective adaptation to time-varying system behaviors. However, such multi-model strategies have rarely been explored in proprioceptive odometry for legged robots, where foot-ground interaction conditions may switch among different contact modes. Despite recent advances, most existing methods still rely on heuristic thresholds, single-model assumptions, or additional sensing to handle contact-induced disturbances, without explicitly modeling multiple contact modes within a unified probabilistic framework. Remark 3. Although the proposed IMM framework can in principle accommodate more contact modes, these additional modes mainly correspond to a finer discretization of the noise scale. In practice, however, closely related modes may not be reliably distinguishable from the available proprioceptive measurements, while increasing the number of modes also leads to higher computational cost. Therefore, a two-mode realization is adopted in this work, consisting of a nominal rolling mode and a slip mode with inflated foot velocity noise, which is generally sufficient for practical applications. 

IV. E XPERIMENTS 

To evaluate the performance of the proposed framework, experiments were conducted in both simulations and real-world settings using the AlienGo quadruped robot under a trot gait. The robot integrates IMUs and joint encoders. The open-source Pinocchio library [19] was utilized to model the kinematics of the legged robot. To evaluate the proposed framework, we compared IMM-PO with several representative baselines, including the KF [9], ESKF [8], invariant extended Kalman filter (IEKF) [4], ESKF-VB [20], KalmanNet [21], and ESKF-R, which denotes the single-model rolling-aware ESKF without IMM-based mode adaptation. D. Detailed Analysis of the KalmanNet Baseline 

To better understand the behavior of the learning-based baseline, we provide additional implementation details and error analysis for KalmanNet. In our task, quadruped pro-prioceptive state estimation involves high-dimensional and strongly coupled dynamics together with time-varying foot-ground interactions. Under such conditions, directly apply-ing the standard KalmanNet training pipeline yields limited training and testing performance, suggesting that the original formulation is insufficient for this challenging setting. KalmanNet employs a two-layer recurrent neural network to estimate the Kalman gain from filtering-related features, rather than directly regressing the robot state. While this design preserves the recursive correction structure of the Kalman filter, it also makes the update quality strongly dependent on the recurrent hidden state. In quadruped proprioceptive odometry, the estimator must cope with long-horizon tempo-ral dependencies, contact-mode transitions, and time-varying disturbances. Under these conditions, a compact recurrent representation may be insufficient to fully capture the complex evolution of foot‚Äìground interaction, leading to biased gain prediction and degraded closed-loop stability, especially on long real-world trajectories. More importantly, unlike model-based estimators with explicit mode-dependent contact un-certainty, KalmanNet adapts the correction implicitly through learned recurrent features, which limits its robustness when rolling and slipping behaviors alternate over time. To improve generalization under limited real-world data, several training enhancements were introduced, including pla-nar yaw augmentation, random horizontal translation perturba-tion, curriculum learning, and learning-rate scheduling. These strategies enlarge the effective data coverage and improve robustness to unseen initial orientations and spatial offsets. In addition to the standard position and attitude losses, we further introduce an eigenvalue-based regularization term to explicitly constrain the recursive stability of the learned filter:

V. C ONCLUSIONS 

This paper presented an IMM-based proprioceptive odome-try for legged robots under dynamic foot-ground interactions. Unlike conventional point-contact formulations, the proposed method distinguishes rolling-induced structured contact mo-tion from slip-induced uncertainty. Specifically, a rolling-aware ESKF was developed by augmenting the foot velocity in the state and introducing a rolling-consistent kinematic update, and was further integrated into an IMM framework for online adaptation of contact confidence through mode-dependent foot velocity uncertainty. The proposed framework was validated in both simulation and real-world experiments. The results show that explicit rolling modeling reduces the bias caused by the conventional zero-velocity contact assumption, while IMM-based multi-model inference improves robustness under slip and uncertain terrain interactions.
